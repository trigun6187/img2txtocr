What Is OCR and How Does It Work?

Optical Character Recognition (OCR) is a technology that analyzes images pixel by pixel to detect shapes that resemble letters and words. Modern OCR engines like Google's Tesseract — which powers Free Text OCR — use machine learning models trained on millions of text samples across dozens of languages. When you upload an image, the OCR engine identifies character patterns, groups them into words, and outputs the recognized text.

The key advantage of browser-based OCR is privacy. Unlike server-side tools that upload your files to the cloud, Free Text OCR runs Tesseract.js directly in your browser. Your images never leave your device — no uploads, no servers, no third-party access. This makes it ideal for sensitive documents like bank statements, medical records, contracts, and personal letters.

Step-by-Step Guide: How to Extract Text from an Image

Step 1: Choose Your Image

Start by preparing the image you want to extract text from. Free Text OCR supports JPG, PNG, WEBP, and PDF files. For best results, use images with:

  • High resolution — at least 200 DPI for printed text, 300 DPI for small fonts
  • Good contrast — dark text on a light background works best
  • Minimal distortion — avoid skewed, warped, or heavily shadowed images
  • Clean backgrounds — busy backgrounds can confuse the OCR engine

You can upload images by dragging and dropping them onto the upload area, clicking to browse your files, or using the Take Photo button on mobile to capture text with your camera.

Step 2: Select Your Language

Free Text OCR supports 100+ languages including English, Spanish, German, French, Portuguese, Russian, Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Arabic, Hindi, and many more. Selecting the correct language dramatically improves accuracy because the OCR engine uses language-specific character sets and dictionaries to validate its guesses.

For documents containing multiple languages, choose the primary language. If you're unsure, English is a safe default for Latin-script documents. The first time you select a language, the tool downloads a small language data file (2–10 MB). After that, it works offline.

Step 3: Configure Image Options

Free Text OCR offers several image enhancement options that can significantly improve accuracy for difficult images:

  • Auto Enhance — automatically adjusts contrast, brightness, and sharpness. Enable this for most images.
  • Grayscale — converts the image to black and white, which often improves OCR on colored backgrounds.
  • Invert Colors — useful for white text on dark backgrounds (common in screenshots of dark-mode apps).
  • Deskew — straightens tilted or rotated text.

If your image has poor lighting or was taken with a phone camera, try enabling Auto Enhance first. For screenshots, Grayscale often produces the best results.

Step 4: Click Extract Text

Once your image is uploaded and settings are configured, click the Extract Text button. The OCR engine processes your image and displays the recognized text in the output area. Processing time depends on image size and complexity but typically takes 5–30 seconds.

After extraction, you'll see statistics including character count, word count, and line count. You can copy the text to your clipboard with one click or download it as a .txt file. Your extraction is automatically saved to the History section for easy access later.

Best Image Formats for OCR

Not all image formats perform equally well for OCR. Here's what you need to know:

  • PNG — Best overall. Lossless compression preserves text clarity. Ideal for screenshots and scanned text.
  • JPG — Good for photographs. However, lossy compression can create artifacts around text edges. Use high-quality JPGs (quality 90+).
  • WEBP — Modern format with excellent compression. Fully supported and performs similarly to PNG.
  • PDF — Supported for documents up to 50 MB. Each page is rendered as an image, then OCR is applied. Multi-page PDFs are handled automatically.

💡 Pro Tip: If you have a PDF that already contains selectable text, you don't need OCR — just copy the text directly. OCR is for image-based PDFs (scanned documents) where the text is part of the image, not a separate text layer.

Handling PDFs vs. Images

When you upload a PDF to Free Text OCR, the tool renders each page as a high-resolution image using PDF.js (the same engine powering Firefox's PDF viewer). It then runs OCR on each rendered page sequentially. This means:

  • Multi-page PDFs work — all pages are processed automatically.
  • Scanned PDFs — perfect use case, since these are essentially images wrapped in a PDF container.
  • Text-based PDFs — for PDFs that already have embedded text, OCR may produce less accurate results than simply copying the text directly. Use a PDF reader for those.

For very large PDFs (40+ pages), consider splitting them into smaller files to avoid browser memory limits. Free Text OCR supports PDFs up to 50 MB.

Tips for Better OCR Accuracy

Getting perfect OCR output depends heavily on input quality. Here are proven tips for maximizing accuracy:

  1. Use 300+ DPI scans — if scanning a document, set your scanner to at least 300 DPI. 600 DPI is even better for small text.
  2. Crop tightly — remove borders, margins, and unrelated graphics that might confuse the engine.
  3. Align text horizontally — Tesseract works best with left-to-right, horizontally aligned text. Skewed text causes errors.
  4. Choose the right language — selecting "English" for a Spanish document will produce gibberish. Always match the language.
  5. Enable Auto Enhance — this single toggle often doubles accuracy for low-quality images.
  6. Avoid handwritten text — Tesseract is designed for printed text. Neat printed handwriting may yield 70–85% accuracy, but cursive script is unreliable.
  7. Check for watermarks — watermarks and overlapping graphics can confuse OCR. Crop them out if possible.

Common OCR Problems and Solutions

Problem: Garbled or nonsensical output

Solution: Check that you selected the correct language. Also verify that your image has sufficient resolution and contrast. Try enabling Auto Enhance.

Problem: OCR takes too long

Solution: Large images (4000+ pixels) take longer to process. Resize images to 2000–3000 pixels wide for faster processing with minimal accuracy loss.

Problem: PDF only shows first page text

Solution: Free Text OCR processes all pages automatically. If you're seeing only one page, the PDF may have a single rendered page. Check the original PDF page count.

Problem: Special characters or symbols not recognized

Solution: Some symbols and non-standard characters may not be in Tesseract's training data for your selected language. For documents with mixed scripts, try processing each script separately.
