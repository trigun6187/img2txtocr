How to Extract Text from Screenshots Using Free OCR Tool

Screenshots are one of the most common sources of text for OCR. Whether you are capturing an error message, a tweet, a slide from a presentation, or a receipt, a screenshot gives you a static image that you can process with an online OCR tool.

Why Screenshots Work Well for OCR

Unlike photos of physical documents, screenshots are digital-born images. They tend to have sharp edges, consistent lighting, and no perspective distortion. This makes them ideal for OCR — most screenshots of typed text achieve 97–99% character accuracy with tools like img2txt.

Best results: Screenshots of black text on a white background, captured at 100% zoom, produce the highest OCR accuracy. Avoid compressed JPEG screenshots — save as PNG instead.

Step-by-Step: Extract Text from a Screenshot

  1. Capture your screenshot — Use Win+Shift+S (Windows), Cmd+Shift+4 (macOS), or Power+Volume-Down (Android). Save it as a PNG file.
  2. Open img2txt in your browser — No sign-up, no install. Works on desktop and mobile.
  3. Upload the screenshot — Click or drag the file into the upload area. The tool accepts JPG, PNG, WebP, and BMP.
  4. Select the language — English is the default. Change it if your screenshot contains other languages.
  5. Enable Auto Enhance — Leave this on (it is checked by default). It automatically adjusts brightness and contrast for optimal OCR.
  6. Choose a layout — "Auto" works for most screenshots. Use "Single Column" for articles or long-form text.
  7. Click "Extract Text" — A loading spinner shows the worker initializing and processing the image.
  8. Copy or download — Once the confidence badge appears, copy the text to your clipboard or download it as a .txt file.
Pro tip: If you OCR the same type of screenshot regularly (e.g. weekly reports), the extraction history feature stores your last 10 results. Click any past entry to restore it.

Common Screenshot OCR Problems & Fixes

  • Blurry text after resizing: Capture at native resolution. img2txt auto-upscales images under 300px.
  • Colored text on colored background: Enable the grayscale toggle or keep Auto Enhance on — it will convert to grayscale when it detects high color noise.
  • Very small text: Increase the brightness slider (or let Auto Enhance handle it).
  • Multi-column layout: Switch the PSM dropdown to "Single Column" or "Single Block" depending on the layout.

Convert PDF Images to Editable Text Online Without Signup

PDFs are everywhere — reports, invoices, scanned books, contracts. But many PDFs contain text as images (scanned pages) rather than selectable text. Extracting that text usually requires expensive software or uploading sensitive documents to a third-party server.

The Problem with Traditional PDF OCR

Most online PDF OCR services require you to:

  • Create an account or sign in with Google
  • Upload the PDF to their cloud servers
  • Wait for server-side processing
  • Download the result — often with watermarks on the free tier

This raises serious privacy concerns when the PDF contains confidential information like contracts, medical records, or financial statements.

The img2txt solution: All processing happens locally in your browser. Your PDF never leaves your device. No upload, no signup, no watermarks.

How to Convert a PDF to Text with img2txt

  1. Open img2txt in any modern browser (Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Safari).
  2. Upload your PDF — Maximum file size is 50 MB. The tool uses PDF.js to render each page as an image client-side.
  3. Select language and layout — Choose the language of your document. "Single Column" works well for most scanned PDFs.
  4. Enable Auto Enhance — Scanned pages often have uneven lighting. Auto Enhance compensates for shadows and faded ink.
  5. Click "Extract Text" — Progress updates show which page is being processed.
For best results with scanned PDFs: Use a scanner resolution of at least 300 DPI. Darken the contrast in your scanner settings if the original document is faded. For multi-page PDFs, img2txt processes every page sequentially.

What to Do with the Extracted Text

  • Copy to clipboard — Paste into Word, Google Docs, or your email.
  • Download as .txt — Save the raw text file for archiving or further processing.
  • Check confidence — The color-coded badge (green/orange/red) tells you how reliable the extraction is. Review orange or red sections manually.

OCR Tools Compared: Tesseract.js vs Commercial APIs

Under the hood, every OCR tool runs on an engine. img2txt uses Tesseract.js, a JavaScript port of Google's Tesseract OCR engine. But how does it compare to commercial APIs like Google Cloud Vision, AWS Textract, and Azure Cognitive Services?

What Is Tesseract.js?

Tesseract.js compiles the Tesseract OCR engine (written in C++) into WebAssembly so it runs directly in the browser. It supports 100+ languages and includes configurable page segmentation modes (PSM). Version 5 (used by img2txt) adds the rotateAuto feature for auto-orientation detection.

When to Choose Tesseract.js

  • Privacy is critical — Medical records, legal documents, or proprietary data that cannot be uploaded to a third party.
  • Budget is zero — Tesseract.js is completely free with no usage limits.
  • No hidden costs — Tesseract.js is free, no API keys, no usage tiers, no paid upgrades.
  • Rapid prototyping — No API keys, no SDK setup, no authentication.

When to Use a Commercial API

  • Handwritten text — Commercial APIs have dedicated handwriting recognition models that outperform Tesseract significantly.
  • Tables and forms — AWS Textract and Google Form Parser extract structured data (key-value pairs, table cells) which Tesseract does not support natively.
  • Very high throughput — Processing millions of documents is faster on dedicated cloud infrastructure than on a browser's single-threaded worker.
  • Multi-language mixed documents — Commercial APIs auto-detect and switch between languages in a single document more reliably.
Bottom line: For 90% of everyday OCR tasks — extracting text from screenshots, scanned documents, signs, and PDFs — Tesseract.js is more than sufficient and has the unmatched advantage of being free and private. Use commercial APIs only when you need handwriting recognition or structured table extraction at scale.

How to Use OCR on Mobile Phone to Extract Text from Photos

Your phone's camera is a powerful OCR input device. With img2txt, you can capture a photo of any text — a restaurant menu, a shipping label, a whiteboard, a page in a book — and extract the text in seconds, all on-device.

Why Use Your Phone Camera for OCR?

  • No scanner needed — A modern phone camera (12 MP or higher) captures enough detail for reliable OCR.
  • Instant capture — No need to save a photo first and then upload it. Capture directly from the tool.
  • Portable — OCR any text you encounter, wherever you are.
Privacy note: When you use the camera capture feature on img2txt, the photo stays on your phone. It is never uploaded to any server.

Step-by-Step: Mobile OCR with img2txt

  1. Open img2txt on your phone — Works in Chrome, Safari, Firefox, and Samsung Internet.
  2. Tap the upload area — On mobile, this opens a file picker with a "Take Photo" option (uses the rear camera by default).
  3. Take a photo of the text — Hold the phone steady. Ensure the text is flat, evenly lit, and fills most of the frame.
  4. Adjust settings — Select the language. Auto Enhance is on by default — it corrects lighting, contrast, and rotation.
  5. Tap "Extract Text" — Processing happens on-device. The loading spinner shows progress.
  6. Copy text — Tap the copy button to paste the text into any app.

Tips for the Best Mobile OCR Results

  • Lighting: Use natural daylight or position a lamp so the text is evenly lit. Avoid direct flash — it creates glare.
  • Distance: Hold the camera about 20–30 cm (8–12 inches) from the page. The text should fill at least half the frame.
  • Angle: Shoot straight-on (parallel to the page). Perspective distortion reduces OCR accuracy. img2txt does not auto-deskew, so keep the camera parallel.
  • Focus: Tap the screen to focus on the text before taking the photo. Blurry photos produce garbage OCR output.
  • Background: Place dark text on a light, solid background. Avoid textured surfaces or patterned backgrounds.

Mobile OCR Use Cases

  • Business cards — Capture a card and extract the name, phone number, and email instantly.
  • Whiteboards — Photograph meeting notes and convert them to searchable text.
  • Restaurant menus — Extract the text to translate it or save it for later.
  • Shipping labels — Record tracking numbers without typing.
  • Books & articles — Capture a page and quote it directly instead of retyping.
Pro tip for iOS: If you are on iOS 15+, you can use Live Text (built into the Photos app) for instant text selection. For Android, img2txt provides the same capability without any OS version requirements — and it works for any language Tesseract supports.
